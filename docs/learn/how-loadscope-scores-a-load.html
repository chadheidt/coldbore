How Loadscope Scores a Load: Flat-Spot, SD, Mean Radius, Vertical SD

Most tools graph your data and leave the call to you. Loadscope scores every load you tested and names your best one — weighting the measurements the way your test distance demands. Here's exactly how.

Four measurements that show up on target

Loadscope scores each charge and seating depth on four things, each put on the same 0-to-1 scale (lower is better) and blended into one composite. Lowest composite wins.

  • Mean radius — the average distance of each shot from group center. A steadier read on true precision than a single group-size number, because one flier doesn't dominate it. It leads the score up close, where the group on paper is the honest read.
  • Velocity SD — shot-to-shot velocity consistency. It puts almost nothing on target at 100 yards, but turns into vertical downrange — so it earns more weight the farther you shoot.
  • Vertical — how much the shots stack up and down. At range this is what a powder charge most directly drives.
  • Flat-spot — how little your average velocity moves across nearby charges. Kept as a light, optional signal — more on why below. (Read more on reading a powder ladder.)

The weights follow your test distance

The four aren't weighted equally, and the mix isn't a fixed table — Loadscope sets it from the distance you tested at. The reason is physics: a powder charge controls velocity, and velocity turns into vertical spread downrange — near zero at 100 yards, steep by 1000. So up close, mean radius leads and velocity barely counts; stretch out and velocity SD takes over. Seating-depth tests lean on mean radius at every distance, because seating tunes group shape, not speed.

Why flat-spot stays light

Barrels do vibrate, and positive compensation is real, measured physics — we don't dispute that. What Loadscope weights lightly is the idea that a three-to-five shot ladder can reliably find a velocity flat-spot; large-sample testing shows that read is mostly sampling noise, and even barrel-tuner researchers don't defend the small-sample version. It stays an optional signal you can weight up — not the thing your winner rides on.

What it can't see

Two honest limits. Wind is the biggest long-range miss, and no load metric captures it — at distance it contaminates your horizontal spread, which is one reason mean radius earns less weight far out. And small samples lie: a five-shot SD of 9 fps has a true range of roughly 5 to 26 fps. Scores are a ranking aid, not proof — more shots make every number more trustworthy.

You can still set the weights yourself

Loadscope sets a sensible starting point, but every weight is a slider. A benchrest shooter chasing the smallest group can lean harder on mean radius; a long-range shooter who lives and dies by vertical can push SD and vertical higher. Change the weights and Loadscope re-scores instantly — or hand the dial back and let it set them from your distance again.

The hard questions

“Velocity SD doesn't matter at 100 yards — why weight it at all?”

Correct that it barely touches the target at 100. We weight it as a load-quality signal that predicts distance performance, not as a 100-yard hit driver — and the weight climbs to dominate at range, exactly where the ballistics say it should. If you only ever shoot close, slide it down; the app lets you.

“Group size should decide the winner.”

We score group quality with mean radius, which uses every shot and is far steadier at small sample sizes than extreme-spread group size. Group size is still shown — it just doesn't set the ranking on the strength of your two worst shots.

“You're ignoring the flat-spot or harmonic tune.”

Barrels do vibrate and positive compensation is real, measured physics — we don't dispute that. What we weight lightly is the idea that a three-to-five shot chronograph ladder reliably finds a velocity flat-spot; large-sample testing shows that read is mostly sampling noise, and even barrel-tuner researchers don't defend the small-sample version. It stays an optional signal you can weight up.

“Who says these exact weights are right?”

Nobody — and we say so. The physics sets the shape and a defensible range; the exact number is our starting point, and every weight is a slider. The honesty is the defense: we can show the model, the sources, and the reasoning behind each choice.
